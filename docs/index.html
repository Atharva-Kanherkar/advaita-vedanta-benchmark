Most models can sound profound. Few can hold a precise position when you argue back.

AdvaitaBench is a closed-book pressure test for doctrinal reliability: can a model keep classical Advaita distinct from neighboring schools, respect levels of reality, and refuse popular misconceptions under multi-turn attack? That failure mode shows up anywhere users ask for careful reasoning — not only in philosophy.

Why this matters

If your model is going to teach, advise, or debate in high-stakes domains, fluency is not enough. You need evidence it can keep distinctions stable when the user pushes.

01Catch confident vagueness before users do Philosophy punishes answers that sound deep while being subtly wrong — the same failure mode that shows up in law, medicine, and policy chat 02Stress-test consistency under pressure Same doctrine asked neutrally, hostilely, academically, and in Sanskrit. If the answer flips, you have a reliability bug 03A signal model makers can actually use Public tasks, hard failure caps, and a bias-corrected two-judge score — so labs can track regressions instead of vibes

How it works

Three design decisions do most of the work. Full methodology, rubrics, and the bias-correction formula are in the blog and the repo.

01Pressure, not trivia Multi-turn debates quote genuine scripture against the model until it holds or folds 02Reference-based grading Judges verify against written reference answers, with hard caps for known failure modes 03Judge bias, measured and corrected Each AI judge favored its own maker's models. We measured it and subtracted it
